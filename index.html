Why this matters: Legitimate healthcare providers typically bill many different procedure codes reflecting diverse patient needs. A provider that bills 99–100% of their claims under a single HCPCS code has a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) near 1.0 — maximum concentration. This pattern is a hallmark of "service mills" that perform the same procedure repeatedly, sometimes regardless of whether patients actually need it.
Important context: Some specializations (e.g., home case management agencies, clinical labs) naturally have limited procedure diversity. However, when combined with high dollar volumes, this becomes a stronger fraud indicator.

Benford's Law Analysis
Do the leading digits of claim amounts follow the expected natural distribution?
What is Benford's Law? In naturally occurring financial data, the leading digit "1" should appear about 30% of the time, "2" about 17.6%, and so on. Fabricated or manipulated numbers tend to deviate from this distribution. Forensic accountants use this as a first-pass fraud screen. Large deviations indicate the data may have been artificially constructed.

Methodology
How these signals were detected

Dataset

227 million rows of Medicaid provider spending data from CMS (2018–2024). Each row contains a billing/servicing NPI pair, HCPCS code, claim month, beneficiary count, claim count, and total paid amount.

7-Signal Risk Model

Composite scoring using cost-per-claim (22%), claims-per-beneficiary (14%), billing volatility/CV (14%), procedure concentration/HHI (12%), cost-per-beneficiary (12%), proxy billing ratio (10%), and total paid (10%).

Statistical Methods

Log-transform IQR for skewed cost distributions, Benford's Law first-digit analysis, Herfindahl-Hirschman Index for procedure concentration, Coefficient of Variation for temporal volatility. All computed via DuckDB over Parquet staging.

Provider Lookups

NPI details resolved from the NPPES NPI Registry (CMS). Provider names, specialties, and locations are pulled in real time to provide context for each flagged entity.

Disclaimer: This analysis identifies statistical anomalies, not confirmed fraud. Many flagged providers may have legitimate explanations for their billing patterns (large institutional entities, specialty services, etc.). This data is intended to inform further investigation, not to make accusations. All data sourced from publicly available CMS Medicaid Provider Utilization data.
